A Sound Atlas of the Qur’an

Every one of the 6,236 verses of the Tanzil text was read and measured: per-surah rhyme signatures, every sustained break in them adjudicated in context, a complete census of refrains and cross-surah formulas, and one honest negative result. The central finding: when this text changes its sound, it is changing its subject — 27 of 27 machine-flagged sound-breaks landed on real structural seams, 21 of them exactly.

IRhyme signatures: each surah keeps its own sound

The verse-final word was extracted for all 6,236 verses and reduced to a pausal rhyme key. Most surahs hold one acoustic signature with remarkable discipline — Surah 91 (ash-Shams) ends all fifteen of its verses on -āhā; Surah 55 (ar-Rahman) holds -ān across 66 of 78 verses; Surah 23 sustains the high-vowel-plus-nasal family for 97% of its 118 verses. The signature is a surah-level structural unit, not an incidental ornament.

IIThe break test: does a sound-shift mark a content pivot?

The hypothesis came from a firsthand reading observation: in Surah Maryam, the surah’s gentle -iyyā rhyme turns to harsh doubled consonants exactly at the denunciation of “the Most Merciful has taken a son.” Generalized and tested: a detector flagged every run of two or more consecutive verses that abandon their surah’s dominant rhyme family (82 blocks corpus-wide, after merging acoustically equivalent families like -ūn/-īn). The 27 strongest cross-family blocks were then read in context and adjudicated, with skepticism instructed and “no correlation” a valid verdict.

Result: 21 clear pivots, 6 partials, 0 misses. Not one block shifted its sound over inert content.

Specimen · Surah 19 (Maryam) — the original finding, sharpened

The detector found two breaks, not one. At 19:34–40 the ‘Isa narrative halts for a doctrinal verdict (“it is not for God to take a son”) and the rhyme snaps out of -iyyā; the instant narration resumes with Ibrahim at 19:41, the rhyme snaps back. The famous harsh turn is the second break — and it begins at 19:75, earlier than eyeball reading suggested, running to the end:

وَقَالُوا اتَّخَذَ الرَّحْمَـٰنُ وَلَدًا ▪ لَّقَدْ جِئْتُمْ شَيْئًا إِدًّا ▪ تَكَادُ السَّمَاوَاتُ يَتَفَطَّرْنَ مِنْهُ

“They say the Most Merciful has taken a son — you have uttered a monstrous thing; the heavens nearly split apart from it” (19:88–90). The soft -iyyā becomes iddā / haddā / ḍiddā — the phonetics turn violent with the content, then soften for the verse about love (19:96, wuddā).

Specimen · Surah 47 — the rhyme is the addressee

This surah rhymes on pronoun suffixes. Verses describing the hypocrites end in -hum (“them”); when the text turns to exhort the believers, the endings turn to -kum (“you”) — at 47:35–38 the change of addressee is literally audible as the change of rhyme.

Specimen · Surah 82 — cosmos to whisper

Five verses of cosmic collapse in cascading feminine perfects (infaṭarat, intatharat, fujjirat, buʿthirat) — then the sound pivots to -ak at the exact verse the camera drops from the universe to one person: يَا أَيُّهَا الْإِنسَانُ مَا غَرَّكَ بِرَبِّكَ الْكَرِيمِ “O man, what deluded you about your generous Lord — who created you, proportioned you, balanced you?”

Four mechanisms, not one

Oath-prologue handoff. Opening oath sequences carry their own rare sound (consonant-final, onomatopoeic) and snap to the body rhyme at the exact verse the argument begins (37, 51, 52, 56).
Coda shift. A surah’s finale takes a heightened ending-sound of its own — intensive divine names, singular adjectives, a closing credo (2:285, 3:189–199, 41:44–54, 76:25–31).
Audible addressee. Where the rhyme is a pronoun suffix, a change of who is being spoken to is a change of sound (47:35–38; 82:6–8).
Sound-brackets. Doctrinal interjections, lyric interludes, and threat-parentheses inside a running mode are fenced by a temporary rhyme, which releases when the frame resumes (19:34–40, 24:36–45, 73:12–13).
Honest limits. The implication is one-directional: a sustained sound-break reliably marks a seam, but most seams (especially in Medinan legal surahs) occur without sound-breaks — rhyme-break ⇒ pivot, not pivot ⇒ rhyme-break. A minority of shifts are partly grammar-driven (accusative -ā endings in 35:39–45). And the adjudicators, though instructed to be skeptical and free to return “no,” knew the hypothesis; a blinded design would be stronger.

The pattern that matters: refrains are local, formulas are global. Within a surah, exact repetition is a deliberate architectural device; across surahs, shared material is almost never copied whole — it is re-inflected each time (275 exact duplicates out of 6,236 verses is a small fraction, and the retold narratives — Musa three ways in Surahs 26–28 — barely reuse sentences). Orbit, not echo, holds at the corpus scale.

VA negative result, reported as one

The claimed ring composition of al-Baqara (its 286 verses said to mirror around the center, near 2:143 “a middle nation”) was tested lexically: similarity of mirror-pair verses (i vs. N+1−i, stopwords removed) against 30 shuffled baselines. Result: 0.0087 vs. 0.0082 ± 0.0016 — no signal. If al-Baqara’s ring structure is real, it lives at the level of theme and pericope, not shared vocabulary; this test cannot confirm it and does not refute the thematic claim. It is recorded here so the atlas contains what was looked for and not found, not only what was found.

VIWhat the atlas adds up to

The sound-layer of this text is not decoration on top of the message; it is a second channel carrying the same structure. Surahs hold acoustic signatures with near-total discipline, and every sustained departure examined turned out to be doing work: fencing a doctrinal verdict, sealing a coda, switching the addressee, bracketing an aside. A listener who understood no Arabic would still hear the seams of the argument. For a text composed orally across two decades and fixed without revision, that is the finding: the form knows what the content is doing, everywhere we checked.
